About COSMOS Analytics

COSMOS Analytics is a cutting-edge machine learning system designed to revolutionize exoplanet verification. Using advanced algorithms trained on NASA's Kepler mission data, our platform analyzes transit photometry to distinguish confirmed exoplanets from false positives with unprecedented accuracy, while simultaneously predicting planetary properties such as radius estimation.

✨ Key Features

Dual Classification

Simultaneous exoplanet classification (Confirmed/False Positive) and planetary radius regression with confidence scores.

11 Transit Parameters

Comprehensive input including orbital period, transit depth, signal-to-noise ratio, and host star properties.

Full Data Persistence

All predictions automatically stored with timestamps and parameters for historical analysis and trending.

Real-Time Validation

Client-side validation ensures all inputs fall within realistic astrophysical ranges before submission.

Interactive Visualizations

Beautiful Chart.js visualizations including confidence meters, probability distributions, and category analysis.

Prediction History

Browse recent analyses with key metrics, track classification trends, and compare planetary discoveries.

⚙️ How It Works

1

Input Transit Data

Provide observational parameters from your Kepler photometry including orbital characteristics and host star properties.

2

Validation & Preprocessing

Client-side validation checks parameter ranges, then data is sent to our ML pipeline for feature engineering and scaling.

3

ML Inference

Advanced classification model determines if the signal indicates a confirmed exoplanet or false positive, with confidence scores.

4

Radius Estimation

Regression model estimates planetary radius in Earth radii and categorizes the planet type (Earth-sized, Super-Earth, Neptune-like, Jupiter-like).

5

Results & Storage

View comprehensive visualizations of your results and automatic database storage enables historical tracking and trend analysis.

📡 Data Source

This system is inspired by and trained on data from NASA's Kepler Space Telescope mission, which discovered thousands of exoplanets through transit photometry. The machine learning models use patterns from confirmed exoplanet detections to classify new candidates with high accuracy.

The Kepler mission has been instrumental in understanding the prevalence and distribution of Earth-sized and smaller planets around other stars, fundamentally advancing our knowledge of planetary systems in the galaxy.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

A Kepler Object of Interest (KOI) is a star observed by the Kepler space telescope that shows periodic dips in brightness, suggesting the presence of a transiting planet. These signals are analyzed and categorized based on the likelihood of being caused by an actual planet versus false positives like binary stars or instrumental effects.

Our models achieve approximately 95% accuracy on validation data, with confidence scores provided for each prediction. The classification model has been trained on thousands of confirmed Kepler objects, while the regression model shows strong correlation with actual planetary radii within 0.5 Earth radii error margin for confirmed exoplanets.

Input parameters include orbital period (days), transit duration (hours), transit depth (ppm), signal-to-noise ratio, star temperature (K), star gravity (log g), star radius (solar radii), equilibrium temperature (K), and insolation flux (Earth flux). Each parameter helps characterize both the transit signal and the host star properties.

All predictions are stored locally in our database for research purposes and to provide you with historical analysis. Your data is never shared with third parties and is only used to improve our models and provide you with insights about your own prediction history.
